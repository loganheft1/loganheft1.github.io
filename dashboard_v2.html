Retrospectives

What went well, what was learned, what changed

RETRO-01Infrastructure
"Bad data that looks clean is the real risk."
What Happened

An upstream provider silently changed a field from monthly to annual income. Our engine multiplied by 12 — giving every customer 12× their actual income. No flag. No notification.

What I Learned

Completeness checks aren't enough. The real threat is data that passes null checks but violates semantic integrity. Statistical drift checks are as critical as null gates.

What Changed

Built the "Floodgate" — configurable integrity checks per field. Now foundational infrastructure across the entire decisioning stack.

RETRO-02Vendor Management
"Vendor problems are often not vendor problems."
What Happened

Experian bureau files arrived late back-to-back. First instinct: escalate the vendor. Actual root cause: a three-party handoff with no SLA governing the first leg.

What I Learned

When something fails at an integration boundary, map the full dependency chain before assuming fault. The bottleneck was internal — not external.

What Changed

Established formal handoff SLAs across all three parties. Now applies to every vendor integration: draw the full process before assigning blame.

RETRO-03Crisis Management
"Reversibility is a feature, not a fallback."
What Happened

During a $40 RAR/account campaign, 25% of decisions timed out. The instinct was to shut down. I made the call to stay live with enhanced monitoring.

What I Learned

Logic failures and infrastructure failures require different responses. Stochastic errors with no demographic skew can be monitored through. Systematic errors require shutdown.

What Changed

Recovered $3–5M in RAR. Built circuit breaker logic into vendor integrations. Now explicitly map rollback paths before every launch.

RETRO-04Prioritization
"Frameworks protect you from your own preferences."
What Happened

Three competing P1s: FEMA API, email integration, and waterfall logging. I personally wanted the logging overhaul most — it scored last on WSJF.

What I Learned

WSJF isn't just prioritization — it's a bias check. The framework separated what I wanted from what the business needed. FEMA was the right call.

What Changed

FEMA delivered first. Logging got a lightweight dbt solution — 80% of the value at 20% of the effort. WSJF is now default for competing P1s.
